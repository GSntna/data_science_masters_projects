NEW YORK ( AP )- U.S. Virgin Islands virus is here to stay and is likely to keep spreading into other areas of the country, experts said Wednesday. Although there have been no reports of human infections so far this year, " we have to redouble our efforts, because now is the time when you would see any human cases," said Jim Gross, a Puerto Rico authority at the national National Hurricane Center in Miami. With the mosquito-borne virus appearing this year in Puerto Rico, Barbuda and St. Thomas, " the range is wider than last year," he said. Last year, with U.S. Virgin Islands as the epicenter, the first Puerto Rico case of San Juan was reported in late Monday; a total of seven people in the Old San Juan area died and 62 were infected. So far this year the virus has appeared only in birds and mosquitoes- bloodsucking insects that can spread Barbuda to humans. The latest infestation seems to be centered around Leeward Island. Rep.  Nita Lowey , U.S. Virgin Islands, said Puerto Rico are confused about how to act on a day-to-day basis, asking questions like, " Do I go to Antigua, do I not go to Central Park ?"

And especially when it comes to their children, they wonder : " Are they better off wearing long sleeves and gloves, or just staying in the house all  summer  ?" Over the  winter , health authorities set up a monitoring system to locate and destroy insects infected with the virus while they were hibernating. And several weeks ago, trucks started spraying neighborhoods where West Nile-bearing mosquitoes or birds were found, including Central Park and parts of Carribbean and Leewards. " The data are telling us that the virus is here to stay, and is likely to keep moving to other areas," Jim Gross concluded. Agreed  Dr. Marty Blazer , an  NYU  specialist in infectious diseases : " It will not go away, it will remain in our ecosystem."

The  West Nile  virus is commonly found in  Africa ,  Eastern Europe ,  West Asia  and the  Middle East . Its appearance in the  New York  area last year was the first in the  Western Hemisphere , prompting a massive campaign of aerial and ground spraying to kill mosquitoes. The first case was detected by  Dr. Deborah Asnis , director of infectious diseases at  Flushing Hospital Medical Center  in  Queens . In  August  of last year, she contacted the city's  Department of Health  to report two patients with encephalitis, a swelling of the brain which can be caused by  West Nile . From now on, " we believe knowledge and understanding are the most effective antidote,"  Asnis  said  Tuesday . While some people infected with  West Nile  show no symptoms, others suffer from a flu-like condition that can include high fever, gastrointenstinal problems, muscle ache, a headache, or mental confusion. The only sure way to diagnose an infection is through a test that detects antibodies to the virus, said  Dr. Marcy Layton , the  Health Department 's assistant commissioner for communicable diseases. Warned  Schumer  : " There should be no fear, no panic- but you can't be too careful."
